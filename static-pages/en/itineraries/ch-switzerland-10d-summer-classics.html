Swiss Summer Road Trip: Lakes, Alps & Scenic Train Journeys

Eleven days across Switzerland from Lucerne to Zurich: medieval bridges, Jungfraujoch at 11,332 feet, the Matterhorn from the Gornergrat, Neanderthal caves in Appenzell, and Lake Zurich.

📅 Best period June, July, August, September

From Lucerne to Zurich via the Lauterbrunnen Valley, the Jungfraujoch, and Zermatt, this eleven-day road trip traverses Switzerland's most spectacular landscapes. Medieval bridges over the Reuss, subterranean waterfalls in the Bernese Oberland, the Eiger's north face viewed from inside a railway tunnel, the Matterhorn at sunset, Appenzell traditions, and Chagall windows: each stop blends verified history, accessible hikes, and alpine panoramas.

  • Lucerne's Chapel Bridge, a 1333 timber span with panels salvaged from the 1993 fire
  • The Lauterbrunnen Valley with its 72 waterfalls, Tolkien's inspiration for Middle-earth
  • The Jungfraujoch, Europe's highest railway station at 11,332 feet, and the Aletsch Glacier
  • The Matterhorn viewed from the Gornergrat and Matterhorn Glacier Paradise
  • The prehistoric Wildkirchli caves and the cliffside Aescher inn
  • Appenzell's Landsgemeinde, open-air democracy by show of hands
  • Chagall's windows in Zurich's Fraumünster

Day

Lucerne

10 photos
Day 1
🔍

Discover

1
The Chapel Bridge (Kapellbrücke), built around 1333, crosses the Reuss River diagonally for 675 feet. Originally part of the city's fortifications, it carried 158 triangular painted panels commissioned in 1614 by chronicler Renward Cysat, illustrating Swiss history and the lives of patron saints Leodegar and Maurice. On August 18, 1993, fire—likely sparked by a cigarette tossed onto a moored boat—destroyed two-thirds of the structure and 86 of the 111 remaining panels. Rebuilt in eight months at a cost of 3.4 million francs, the bridge reopened on April 14, 1994. Today, roughly 13,800 people cross it daily.
⏱️ 30 min
2
The octagonal Water Tower (Wasserturm) predates the bridge itself: constructed after 1262, probably under Abbot Berthold von Steinbrunn of Murbach, it served variously as prison, torture chamber, treasury, and municipal archives over its 700-year history.
⏱️ 15 min
3
The Lion Monument (Löwendenkmal), carved into the wall of a former sandstone quarry, measures 33 feet long by 20 feet high. Designed by Danish sculptor Bertel Thorvaldsen and executed by stonemason Lukas Ahorn between 1820 and 1821, it commemorates the roughly 760 Swiss Guards killed on August 10, 1792, while defending the Tuileries Palace in Paris during the French Revolution. The Latin inscription 'Helvetiorum Fidei ac Virtuti' honors their loyalty. In 1880, Mark Twain called it 'the saddest and most moving piece of stone in the world.' The monument was initiated by Karl Pfyffer von Altishofen, a Guards officer who was on leave in Lucerne the day of the massacre. Around 1.4 million visitors come annually to stand before the dying lion.
⏱️ 30 min
4
The Spreuer Bridge (Spreuerbrücke), Lucerne's second covered bridge, built in 1408, houses a cycle of 67 'Dance of Death' paintings created by Kaspar Meglinger between 1626 and 1635. Unlike the Chapel Bridge, these panels survived intact.
⏱️ 20 min
+2 Places visited
🎯

Activities

→
Lakefront stroll from the Seebrücke to the Hofkirche (St. Leodegar's Church), a Renaissance basilica built 1633–1639 on the foundations of an 8th-century Benedictine monastery. Its organ of 4,950 pipes, installed in 1648, remains one of Switzerland's oldest still in use.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Cruise on Lake Lucerne (Vierwaldstättersee) aboard historic paddle steamers, some dating from the early 20th century. The route from Lucerne to Flüelen (at the lake's southern tip) takes roughly 3 hours and passes the Rütli Meadow, symbolic birthplace of the Swiss Confederation in 1291.
⏱️ 90 min
→
Wander the Old Town's narrow lanes (Altstadt): frescoed facades tell the stories of guilds and merchant families. The Weinmarkt, Hirschenplatz, and Kornmarkt squares preserve Renaissance fountains and carved oriel-windowed houses.
⏱️ 60 min

Day

Wengen

9 photos
Day 2
🔍

Discover

1
The Lauterbrunnen Valley, a deep U-shaped glacial trough flanked by near-vertical 1,000-foot cliffs, counts 72 waterfalls over five miles. In 1911, a 19-year-old J.R.R. Tolkien hiked this valley during a Swiss journey—he would later draw on its landscapes for Middle-earth, especially Rivendell in 'The Lord of the Rings.'
⏱️ 30 min
2
Staubbach Falls, at the village entrance, plunges 974 feet in free fall—Switzerland's highest. Its name ('dust brook') comes from a thermal updraft that disperses the water into mist before it reaches the ground. In 1779, Goethe, mesmerized by the sight, composed his poem 'Gesang der Geister über den Wassern' (Song of the Spirits over the Waters), helping make the valley famous across Europe. In summer, a path leads behind the falls (free access).
⏱️ 45 min
3
Wengen, first mentioned in 1268, perches on a terrace 4,180 feet above the Lauterbrunnen Valley. The village bans cars and is accessible only by the Wengernalp Railway (WAB), a rack railway in service since 1893. The first inn opened in 1835 at Wengernalp Pass, but tourism truly took off with the train. The Belle Époque hotels downtown recall the golden age of British alpinism.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Trümmelbach Falls, two miles from the village, form Europe's largest subterranean waterfalls. Ten cascades fed by meltwater from the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau glaciers thunder inside the mountain, accessible since 1877 via a lift (330 feet up), then a network of 2,000 feet of galleries, tunnels, and platforms spanning 460 feet of elevation. Flow can reach 5,300 gallons per second, carrying over 20,000 tons of gravel annually.
⏱️ 90 min
→
Wengen-to-Lauterbrunnen descent trail (3.7 miles, –1,640 feet, 1.5 hours): the path winds through woods and pastures with plunging views over the waterfall-studded valley. The WAB train climbs back up in 14 minutes.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Day

Wengen

9 photos
Day 3
🔍

Discover

1
Jungfraujoch houses Europe's highest railway station at 11,332 feet. The Jungfrau Railway, launched in 1896 by Zurich entrepreneur Adolf Guyer-Zeller, took 16 years to bore 4.3 miles of tunnel through the Eiger and Mönch. The first train reached the Jungfraujoch on August 1, 1912. En route, the midpoint Eigerwand station, carved into the Eiger's north face, offers a viewing window straight into the wall.
⏱️ 30 min
2
The Sphinx Observatory terrace (11,716 feet), reached by high-speed elevator from the station, delivers a panorama over the Aletsch Glacier—the Alps' longest at roughly 14 miles—and, on clear days, views extending to the Vosges and Black Forest. The site is part of the Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage area.
⏱️ 45 min
3
The Ice Palace (Eispalast), hollowed 65 feet beneath the glacier surface, displays ice sculptures in chambers held at a constant 27°F. The first tunnels were hand-chiseled in 1934 by two mountain guides who labored for months.
⏱️ 30 min
4
Kleine Scheidegg (6,762 feet), the pass between the Eiger and the Lauberhorn, is accessible by train from Wengen (25 minutes). It's the departure point for the Jungfrau Railway to the Jungfraujoch (11,332 feet). The panorama facing the Eiger's north face is one of the Bernese Oberland's most iconic.
⏱️ 30 min
🎯

Activities

→
Walk the snow plateau (Snow Fun Park) at Jungfraujoch Pass: sledding, zip line, and cross-country skiing on year-round snow. The high altitude causes rapid breathlessness—take it slow.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Round-trip train journey Wengen–Kleine Scheidegg–Jungfraujoch: the rack railway climbs through tunnels bored into the Eiger and Mönch, with a stop at Eigerwand station (9,400 feet) to peer at the north face through windows cut in the rock. Count roughly 5 hours for the complete excursion.
⏱️ 60 min

Day

Wengen

9 photos
Day 4
🔍

Discover

1
Ride the gondola to First (7,113 feet), then walk the First Cliff Walk, a metal gangway suspended from the cliff face, extended by a single-cable bridge and a platform jutting 150 feet over the void. Opened in 2015, the installation offers direct views of the Eiger and Wetterhorn. The gondola ride from the village takes 25 minutes, with two intermediate stations (Bort and Schreckfeld).
⏱️ 60 min
2
From First, the trail leads in 1.5 hours to Bachalpsee (7,431 feet), an alpine lake that, in still weather, mirrors the Schreckhorn and Wetterhorn summits. The lake sits in a wetland of national importance. The trail is moderately difficult with no technical sections.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Iseltwald, a tiny fishing village on the south shore of Lake Brienz, became a global sensation after the Korean series 'Crash Landing on You' filmed scenes on its wooden jetty. The village installed a turnstile at the dock entrance to manage visitor flow. Beyond the phenomenon, Iseltwald remains a quiet hamlet of timbered houses and a small lakeside church.
⏱️ 30 min
🎯

Activities

→
The First Flyer, a 2,625-foot zip line, propels riders at 52 mph over the pastures from First station. The First Glider, shaped like an eagle, flies four passengers horizontally above the landscape.
⏱️ 30 min
→
Mountain-kart descent from Schreckfeld station: these three-wheeled karts hurtle down alpine slopes on a 1.9-mile track to Bort. Open to ages 10+, no license required.
⏱️ 30 min
→
Outfitters offer kayaking or boat trips on Lake Brienz, whose turquoise waters rank among Switzerland's clearest. Optional add-on: Giessbach Falls, accessible by boat then historic funicular (inaugurated 1879, Switzerland's oldest still running), cascade down 14 tiers over 1,640 feet through forest. A trail passes behind the falls.
⏱️ 60 min

Zermatt

10 photos
Day 5
🔍

Discover

1
Zermatt, car-free for decades—only electric vehicles circulate—nestles at the head of the Matter Valley facing the Matterhorn (14,692 feet), whose pyramidal silhouette is visible from virtually every street. The name 'Matterhorn' appears for the first time in 1682; it derives from 'Matte' (the grassy plain at the valley bottom, 'zur Matt') and 'Horn' (peak). Locals simply call it 'ds'Horu.'
⏱️ 45 min
2
The Matterhorn Museum–Zermatlantis, laid out underground like an archaeological site, recreates 19th-century Zermatt and chronicles the conquest of the Matterhorn. Centerpiece: the broken rope from the first ascent on July 14, 1865, displayed in the 'Matterhorn' hall. That day, the seven-man rope team led by Englishman Edward Whymper reached the summit at 1:40 PM via the Hörnli Ridge, beating an Italian team under Jean-Antoine Carrel by three days. But on the descent, the rope snapped: four climbers (Croz, Hadow, Hudson, and Douglas) fell down the north face. Lord Douglas's body was never found.
⏱️ 60 min
3
The Mountaineers' Cemetery, adjacent to St. Peter's Anglican Church, gathers the graves of those who perished on the peaks around Zermatt. Since 1865, nearly 600 people have died on the Matterhorn alone—an average of four per year. Headstones often bear simply a carved rope and ice axe.
⏱️ 20 min
🎯

Activities

→
Five Lakes hike from Sunnegga (reachable by underground funicular in 3 minutes): the loop passes Stellisee, Grindjisee, Grünsee, Moosjisee, and Leisee, each offering a different reflection of the Matterhorn depending on the hour and light.
⏱️ 180 min
→
Stroll down Zermatt's Bahnhofstrasse and along the Vispa stream: old mazots (larch granaries on stilts) and raccard barns of the historic village contrast with modern boutiques and hotels. The Kirchbrücke offers one of the most photographed framings of the Matterhorn.
⏱️ 45 min

Zermatt

10 photos
Day 6
🔍

Discover

1
The Gornergrat rack railway, inaugurated in 1898, climbs in 33 minutes from Zermatt (5,262 feet) to the Gornergrat platform (10,135 feet). It was Switzerland's first open-air electric rack railway. At the summit, the panorama embraces 29 peaks over 13,000 feet, including Monte Rosa (15,203 feet, Switzerland's highest), the Matterhorn, and the Gorner Glacier stretching roughly 7.5 miles down the valley.
⏱️ 60 min
2
The first woman to summit the Matterhorn was Englishwoman Lucy Walker in 1871, at age 34, dressed in a flannel skirt and accompanied by guide Melchior Anderegg. Zermatt guide Ulrich Inderbinen, who lived to 103, reached the top more than 370 times and made his last ascent at 90.
⏱️ 15 min
🎯

Activities

→
Descent on foot from the Gornergrat via the Panorama Trail: photo stops at Rotenboden (views of the Riffelsee reflecting the Matterhorn) and Riffelberg, then down to Zermatt. This 5.6-mile, 4,900-foot descent takes 3 to 4 hours.
⏱️ 210 min
→
Visit the Gorner Gorge (Gornerschlucht): a pathway of walkways and staircases winds 650 feet above the tumbling waters of the Gornervispa.
⏱️ 45 min

Zermatt

10 photos
Day 7
🔍

Discover

1
Matterhorn Glacier Paradise, reached by the tri-cable Matterhorn Glacier Ride I and II gondolas, tops out at 12,739 feet—Europe's highest cable-car station. The observation platform overlooks 38 peaks above 13,000 feet and 14 glaciers, with views spanning three countries (Switzerland, Italy, France). The Glacier Palace, carved 50 feet beneath the ice surface, displays sculptures and crystals in chambers held at a constant 23°F.
⏱️ 120 min
🎯

Activities

→
Outfitters offer tandem paragliding flights above Zermatt, with views of the Matterhorn and surrounding glaciers (seasonal activity, weather-dependent).
⏱️ 60 min
→
Evening stroll down the Bahnhofstrasse and pause on a terrace: the Kirchbrücke frames Zermatt's most photographed Matterhorn shot, especially in late afternoon when the light turns the pyramid pink.
⏱️ 60 min

Appenzell

7 photos
Day 8
🔍

Discover

1
The town of Appenzell (population ~7,000), capital of Canton Appenzell Innerrhoden, retains a car-free core of frescoed gable facades. The Hauptgasse (main street) lines up painted houses depicting pastoral and religious scenes. Step inside the Löwen apothecary, whose facade adorned with medicinal plants by painter Johannes Hugentobler is considered the village jewel. The Town Hall bears frescoes by August Schmid dating from 1928–1930, representing the eight ages of life.
⏱️ 60 min
2
On the Landsgemeindeplatz, the central square, the Landsgemeinde convenes every last Sunday in April—an open-air assembly where citizens vote by show of hands on cantonal affairs. This centuries-old form of direct democracy survives only in Appenzell Innerrhoden and Glarus. Women were admitted only in 1991, after a Federal Court ruling—it was the last Swiss canton to grant them the vote.
⏱️ 20 min
🎯

Activities

→
Taste Appenzeller cheese, handcrafted in the region for over 700 years (first mentioned in 1282, when peasants used it to pay tithes to the monks of St. Gallen Abbey). Each wheel is rubbed for at least three months with 'Sulz,' a secret brine based on mountain herbs whose exact recipe only two people per generation know. The cheese comes in classic (3 months), surchoix (4–6 months), and extra (6–8 months).
⏱️ 45 min
→
Evening wander through Appenzell's lanes: the wrought-iron shop signs, carved wooden balconies, and multicolored facades are especially photogenic in late-day light. The Chapel of the Holy Cross, rebuilt in 1560, marks the historic edge of the center.
⏱️ 30 min

Appenzell

7 photos
Day 9
🔍

Discover

1
From Wasserauen, the Ebenalp cable car (in service since 1955) climbs 2,372 feet in six minutes to reach the summit at 5,394 feet. From the station, a trail descends in 15 minutes through the Wildkirchli caves, a network of three interconnected caverns between 4,882 and 4,921 feet in the cliff face. In 1904, naturalist Emil Bächler discovered flaked flints and cave-bear bones—the world's first proof of Neanderthal presence in the mountains, dated to roughly 40,000 BCE. The largest cave, the Altarhöhle, was fitted out as a chapel dedicated to St. Michael in 1657.
⏱️ 45 min
2
Between 1658 and 1853, hermits inhabited the Wildkirchli caves during the summer months. Curate Paulus Ulmann was the first to settle there. The small hermitage, restored in 1972, now houses a museum presenting the prehistoric finds, including remains of some 800 animals—cave bears, cave lions, and wolves.
⏱️ 20 min
3
The Berggasthaus Aescher, tucked into the rock wall of the Ebenalp at 4,770 feet, has stood in its present form since 1860. Originally built by farmers, it's one of Switzerland's oldest mountain inns. One of its walls is the rock itself. With no road access, it receives 1,760 pounds of supplies per week, sometimes by helicopter. The Ebenalp draws over 200,000 visitors annually.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Hike Ebenalp–Seealpsee–Wasserauen: from the Wildkirchli caves and the Aescher inn, the trail descends in roughly 45 minutes to Seealpsee (3,750 feet), a 33.6-acre alpine lake cradled between the Alpstein walls. Its calm waters mirror the Säntis and surrounding peaks. The lake loop takes about an hour. The descent to Wasserauen adds 1.5 miles and 985 feet of drop.
⏱️ 210 min

Zurich

7 photos
Day 10
🔍

Discover

1
The Niederdorf, nicknamed 'Dörfli' by locals, is the pedestrian heart of the right bank: narrow medieval lanes, half-timbered houses, wrought-iron shop signs, and a concentration of restaurants, cafés, and theaters. It was here that the Dada movement was born at Cabaret Voltaire in 1916, founded by Hugo Ball and Emmy Hennings during World War I.
⏱️ 60 min
2
The Lindenhof, a linden-shaded hill between the Old Town's two quarters, occupies the site of a Roman castrum (Turicum). Excavations have uncovered the fort's foundations. Today, this peaceful, elevated square offers one of the best views over the Limmat, the Niederdorf, and the Grossmünster's twin towers.
⏱️ 20 min
3
The Bahnhofstrasse, 0.9 miles long, links the main station (Hauptbahnhof) to the lake. Laid out in 1864 atop the old city moat, it became one of the world's most expensive shopping streets. Beneath the street, bank vaults are rumored to hold a significant share of the world's gold.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Lakefront promenade from Bürkliplatz, where the Saturday flea market unfolds. On clear days, the Glarus Alps rise on the horizon. In summer, public baths built directly onto the lake or the Limmat have been a Zurich tradition since the 19th century.
⏱️ 60 min
→
Fondue dinner in an Old Town restaurant or in the Zürichberg hills: Zurich tradition holds that you dip your bread clockwise and that whoever loses their chunk buys a bottle.
⏱️ 90 min

Zurich

7 photos
Day 11
🔍

Discover

1
The Grossmünster, begun in the 12th century, dominates the right bank of the Limmat with its twin towers. Legend holds that Charlemagne discovered the tombs of martyred saints Felix and Regula on this spot. From this church, Ulrich Zwingli launched the Protestant Reformation in Zurich in 1519, profoundly transforming the city's religious and political life. You can climb the south tower (187 steps) for a panorama over the Old Town and lake.
⏱️ 45 min
2
Cross the Limmat to reach the Fraumünster, a former abbey church founded in 853 by King Louis the German for his daughter Hildegard. The abbey held the right to mint coins, collect market taxes, and raise troops, making it Zurich's dominant power for centuries. The chief attraction today: five choir windows created by Marc Chagall in 1970, each dedicated to a biblical theme in shades of blue, green, red, yellow, and blue-pink.
⏱️ 30 min
3
The Kunsthaus, founded in 1787, holds Switzerland's most important art collection: medieval works to contemporary, with major holdings of Munch, Giacometti, Monet, and Picasso. The extension designed by David Chipperfield, opened in 2021, doubled the exhibition space.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Final stroll through the Old Town lanes: Cabaret Voltaire at Spiegelgasse 1, birthplace of Dada in 1916, still operates as a bar and cultural space. A few streets over, Albert Einstein lived at Spiegelgasse 14 during his Zurich years.
⏱️ 60 min

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Perfect weather for hiking, warmest temperatures, peak tourist season, highest prices, long daylight hours, festival season.

Winter (December-March): World-class skiing, Christmas markets, winter sports paradise, expensive ski resorts, shorter daylight, magical snowy landscapes.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather, fewer crowds, moderate prices, wildflower blooms, excellent for cultural sites, unpredictable mountain weather.

Fall (September-October): Pleasant temperatures, autumn colors, harvest season, good weather, fewer tourists, excellent value before ski season.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 9 and 13 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Switzerland?

Alpine perfection with pristine mountains, luxury watches, and world-famous chocolate culture. Experience efficient trains, medieval towns, and outdoor adventures in Europe's playground. The country offers incredible hiking, skiing, and luxury experiences with stunning mountain scenery. Switzerland provides reliability, beauty, and luxury in compact Alpine paradise.
