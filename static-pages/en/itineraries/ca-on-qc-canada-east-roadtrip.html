Eastern Canada Road Trip: Toronto, Niagara Falls & Québec City

Where waterfalls thunder at the border and whale spouts pierce the St. Lawrence, where Québec's ramparts have stood watch for four centuries—eastern Canada unfolds a gripping natural and historical spectacle from Toronto to the Saguenay Fjord.

📅 Best period June, July, August, September

From Toronto to Tadoussac, this road trip crosses eastern Canada in 14 days. Two nights in Toronto to explore multicultural neighborhoods and the 553-meter CN Tower. Niagara Falls and the Loyalist village of Niagara-on-the-Lake. Ottawa, the federal capital, with Parliament Hill and the UNESCO-listed Rideau Canal. Montréal, its 1829 Notre-Dame Basilica and Olmsted-designed Mount Royal. Mont-Tremblant Park, Québec's oldest. La Mauricie and its 150 lakes. Québec City, the only walled city in North America north of Mexico. Charlevoix, a biosphere reserve, with the Acropole des Draveurs. And finally, the whales of Tadoussac and the Saguenay Fjord.

  • Toronto: world neighborhoods and 553 m CN Tower
  • Niagara: Horseshoe Falls, 57 m drop and 670 m wide
  • Ottawa: Parliament Hill and UNESCO Rideau Canal
  • Montréal: Notre-Dame Basilica and Olmsted's Mount Royal
  • Québec City: Château Frontenac and colonial fortifications
  • Charlevoix: Acropole des Draveurs at 1,048 m
  • Tadoussac: whales and Saguenay Fjord

Toronto

7 photos
Day 1
🔍

Discover

1
Kensington Market traces its roots to waves of Jewish, Portuguese, and Caribbean immigrants who transformed Victorian row houses into a sprawling open-air market. Its rainbow-painted homes now shelter vintage clothing shops, global groceries, and fiercely independent cafés. Adjacent Chinatown stretches along Spadina Avenue with herbalist shops, dim sum parlors, and Mandarin signage—one of North America's largest Chinese quarters thriving since the 1870s.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Graffiti Alley runs for over a kilometer between Portland Street and Bathurst Street, cutting through the heart of Queen West. Every wall wears monumental murals refreshed annually by local and international street artists. Continue onto Queen Street West itself, Toronto's bohemian artery lined with galleries, vinyl record shops, and boutiques showcasing Canadian designers.
⏱️ 75 min
3
The Distillery District occupies the former Gooderham & Worts distillery, founded in 1832 by two English immigrants. By the 1860s, this operation was producing over two million gallons of whisky annually—the largest distillery in the British Empire. Shuttered in 1990, purchased in 2001, it reopened in 2003 as a pedestrian quarter: more than 40 Victorian red-brick buildings, designated a National Historic Site, now house art galleries, glassblowing studios, and microbreweries.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Street food tasting through Kensington Market: tacos, Jamaican patties, empanadas, and fresh-pressed juices from vendors lining the narrow streets.
⏱️ 45 min

Toronto

7 photos
Day 2
🔍

Discover

1
The CN Tower soars 553 meters above Lake Ontario. Built between 1973 and 1976 by Canadian National to solve radio interference caused by downtown skyscrapers, it held the title of world's tallest freestanding structure for over 30 years. More than 1,500 workers labored for 40 months. The Sikorsky helicopter "Olga" hoisted the antenna's 39 sections to the summit. The glass floor at 342 meters suspends visitors above the void. On clear days, the view reaches into New York State.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Catch the ferry at Jack Layton Terminal (departures every 15 minutes in season) to the Toronto Islands. Ward's Island preserves a cottage village inhabited year-round since 1880, with gardens and shaded pathways. Centre Island offers beaches facing Lake Ontario and unobstructed skyline views. The archipelago forms a 4-kilometer chain of 15 entirely car-free islands.
⏱️ 120 min
3
The Royal Ontario Museum, founded in 1912, houses over 13 million objects. Its modern crystal facade of aluminum and glass—the "Michael Lee-Chin Crystal"—was added in 2007. Collections of ancient Chinese art, dinosaur fossils, and minerals rank among the world's finest. Alternative: the Art Gallery of Ontario, redesigned by Frank Gehry in 2008, featuring a 180-meter wood-and-glass galleria suspended above the street.
⏱️ 120 min
🎯

Activities

→
Evening in Koreatown along Bloor Street West between Christie and Bathurst. Korean barbecue restaurants, karaoke bars, and soju lounges line several blocks. Alternative: Little Italy on College Street for sidewalk terraces and espresso cafés.
⏱️ 60 min

Niagara Falls

4 photos
Day 3
🔍

Discover

1
Niagara Falls on the Canadian side delivers the most dramatic view of Horseshoe Falls: 57 meters high, roughly 670 meters across the crest line. About 2,800 cubic meters of water per second are permitted to flow over the falls during daytime in tourist season—at night and off-season, up to 75% of the flow is diverted to hydroelectric plants. Approximately 90% of the visible flow passes over Horseshoe Falls. Erosion pushes the falls upstream about 30 centimeters annually. 12,500 years ago, they stood 11 kilometers downstream at Queenston.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Journey Behind the Falls: tunnels carved through bedrock behind the water curtain lead to two observation decks one-third of the way down Horseshoe Falls. You feel the vibration of the water mass and permanent mist. A yellow raincoat is provided—it will be soaked through.
⏱️ 45 min
3
Niagara-on-the-Lake, 25 kilometers north of the falls, is a former Loyalist town founded in 1781 by refugees from the American Revolution. Named Newark, it served as the first capital of Upper Canada from 1792 to 1796. Razed by Americans during the War of 1812, it was rebuilt in remarkably preserved Georgian and Victorian style. Its historic core, anchored by Queen Street with old-fashioned shops, has been a National Historic Site since 2004.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Boat cruise to the base of the falls: vessels approach within meters of Horseshoe Falls, enveloped in permanent spray and deafening thunder. Duration approximately 20 minutes on the water. Poncho provided.
⏱️ 45 min
→
Ice wine tasting at Niagara Peninsula vineyards. The region counts over 70 wineries. Ice wine is crafted from grapes harvested frozen on the vine at temperatures of -8°C or colder. The peninsula benefits from a microclimate tempered by Lakes Ontario and Erie.
⏱️ 60 min

Ottawa

2 photos
Day 4
🔍

Discover

1
Parliament Hill commands a limestone promontory above the Ottawa River. The current Victorian Gothic Revival buildings date from 1859. After the 1916 fire, the Centre Block was reconstructed and crowned with the Peace Tower, rising 92.2 meters, inaugurated in 1927 to commemorate Canadian soldiers who died in World War I. Its carillon of 53 bells, the largest weighing 10 tons, rings daily. The Memorial Chamber at the tower's base contains the names of every Canadian soldier fallen in combat.
⏱️ 90 min
2
The Rideau Canal, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stretches 202 kilometers between Ottawa and Kingston. Built from 1826 to 1832 under Lieutenant-Colonel John By, it was designed to provide an alternative military route after the War of 1812 in case of American attack. Over 6,000 workers, mainly Irish immigrants and French Canadians, excavated the canal—roughly 1,000 died from malaria, dynamite accidents, or cave-ins. In winter, Ottawa's section becomes the world's longest naturally frozen skating rink.
⏱️ 60 min
3
ByWard Market, operating since 1826, is one of Canada's oldest public markets. Its open-air stalls occupy several blocks around George Street and ByWard Street. Québec cheeses, maple syrup, Ottawa Valley farm products, Indigenous crafts. The surrounding neighborhood teems with restaurants and bars.
⏱️ 75 min
4
The National Gallery of Canada occupies a granite-and-glass building designed by Moshe Safdie, inaugurated in 1988. Its glass-walled Great Hall frames Parliament Hill directly. It houses the country's most important Canadian and Indigenous art collections, plus a remarkable Inuit collection—soapstone carvings, prints, and textiles.
⏱️ 90 min

Montreal

10 photos
Day 5
🔍

Discover

1
Place d'Armes is Montréal's historic heart. At its center, the statue of Maisonneuve, the city's founder in 1642, faces Notre-Dame Basilica. Surrounding the square: the Old Seminary of Saint-Sulpice (1685), the oldest building still standing in Montréal, and the Bank of Montréal tower (1847), Canada's first bank.
⏱️ 45 min
2
Notre-Dame Basilica, built from 1824 to 1829 by Irish-born architect James O'Donnell from New York, is Canada's first Gothic Revival church. It could accommodate up to 10,000 worshippers—for 50 years it was the largest church in North America, all denominations included. The interior, renovated from 1872 to 1880 by architect Victor Bourgeau, is a profusion of carved wood, gilding, and deep blue. The great bell "Jean-Baptiste," installed in 1848, weighs 10,900 kilograms. O'Donnell, born Protestant, converted to Catholicism before his death in 1830; he rests in the crypt of the church he designed.
⏱️ 75 min
3
The Old Port extends 2.5 kilometers along the St. Lawrence River. A former industrial port zone fallen into disuse, it was redeveloped between 1983 and 1992 as a public promenade. Converted warehouses house the Montréal Science Centre. Offshore on Île Sainte-Hélène stands the Biosphere—the former U.S. pavilion from Expo 67, a geodesic dome designed by Buckminster Fuller.
⏱️ 60 min
4
Rue Saint-Paul, Montréal's oldest street, runs through Old Montréal between Place Royale and Bonsecours Market. The latter, built in 1847 in neoclassical style with its silvered dome, served as public market, city hall, and even parliament of the United Province of Canada. Cobblestones, cast-iron lampposts, and grey stone facades give the quarter a European atmosphere.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

Montreal

10 photos
Day 6
🔍

Discover

1
Mount Royal Park, designed by Frederick Law Olmsted (the same landscape architect as New York's Central Park), opened in 1876. The Olmsted Road, the park's main trail, winds gently upward through 200 hectares of urban forest to the Kondiaronk Belvedere, named for the Wendat chief who signed the Great Peace of Montréal with the French in 1701. The view sweeps across downtown, the St. Lawrence River, and on clear days, the Adirondack Mountains to the south. The Mount Royal Chalet, in Beaux-Arts style, displays paintings by Québec artists like Marc-Aurèle Fortin and Paul-Émile Borduas. The summit cross, standing 31 meters tall, was erected in 1924.
⏱️ 120 min
2
The Plateau Mont-Royal is recognizable by its exterior spiral staircases, characteristic of Montréal housing built between 1880 and 1930—a solution to maximize interior space on narrow lots. Avenue Mont-Royal, the neighborhood's commercial artery, alternates independent cafés, used bookstores, and sidewalk terraces. The quarter was home to francophone artists and writers for decades.
⏱️ 75 min
3
Mile End is Montréal's creative quarter, wedged between Boulevard Saint-Laurent and Avenue du Parc. Its former textile workshops have been converted into artist studios and tech startups. Here unfolds the rivalry between Montréal's two bagel bakeries, wood-firing around the clock since the 1950s—bagels smaller, denser, and sweeter than New York's, hand-rolled and poached in honey water. The neighborhood's walls wear over 300 murals—Montréal counts among the world's street art capitals.
⏱️ 75 min
🎯

Activities

→
Wood-fired bagel tasting in Mile End, followed by coffee at one of the neighborhood's independent roasters.
⏱️ 30 min

Mont Tremblant

10 photos
Day 7
🔍

Discover

1
Tremblant's pedestrian village, at the mountain's base, is an ensemble of colorful buildings inspired by traditional Québec villages, constructed from 1991 onward. Access via a free gondola from the parking area. Pedestrian lanes climb in tiers to the ski base, with shops, restaurants, and terraced patios.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Hiking in Mont-Tremblant National Park, Québec's oldest and largest park (1,510 km²), created in 1895—only 23 years after Yellowstone. The Algonquins called it "Manitonga Soutana," the Mountain of Spirits, perceiving rumbling sounds from within. The La Roche and La Corniche trail (8.7 km loop) offers panoramas over the glacial valley of Lac Monroe and the Mont-Tremblant massif, which peaks at 875 meters. The park contains 400 lakes, 6 rivers, and 40 mammal species, including wolves.
⏱️ 240 min
→
Via ferrata du Diable: secured route on the rock face of Vache Noire, at the entrance to the Diable sector. Beams, monkey bridges, and suspended walkways above the Diable River, 200 meters up. Groups of 8 maximum, guided.
⏱️ 150 min

Mauricie

10 photos
Day 8
🔍

Discover

1
La Mauricie National Park, created in 1970, protects 536 km² of Canadian Shield—rock formations among the continent's oldest, formed a billion years ago. Glaciers sculpted the rounded hills and carved over 150 lakes connected by portages. The Promenade Road crosses the park for 63 kilometers from entrance to entrance, through mixed forest of sugar maples, yellow birches, and balsam firs. Indigenous rock paintings were discovered on a cliff at Lac Wapizagonke, 17 kilometers long.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Lac-Solitaire trail hike: gradual ascent through mature maple forest, punctuated by lookouts with plunging views over surrounding lakes. At the summit, a solar-heated warming cabin offers shelter. The park shelters 50 mammal species (eastern wolf, black bear, moose, beaver) and 180 bird species, including the common loon, the park's emblem.
⏱️ 180 min
→
Canoeing on one of the park's 150 lakes. Rentals available at Shewenegan, Wapizagonke, or Lac-Édouard picnic areas. Before 1970, this territory was exploited by logging companies and private hunting and fishing clubs reserved for an anglophone elite. The park ended these privileges to open nature to all.
⏱️ 120 min

Quebec

6 photos
Day 9
🔍

Discover

1
Place Royale, in the Lower Town, marks the exact spot where Samuel de Champlain built his first habitation on July 3, 1608. Notre-Dame-des-Victoires church, erected in 1688, is the oldest stone church in Canada. The gabled houses bordering the square, with steep roofs and stone walls, evoke a Norman village. Champlain chose this site for the river's narrowing, which offered a natural defensive advantage—"Kebec" means "where the river narrows" in Algonquin.
⏱️ 75 min
2
The Petit-Champlain quarter, at the cliff's base, is linked to the Upper Town by the funicular (inaugurated 1879) or the Breakneck Stairs. Its cobbled streets, among North America's oldest, shelter artisan boutiques, leather workshops, and cafés. In winter, decorations transform the quarter into a Christmas village.
⏱️ 60 min
3
Château Frontenac, designed by American architect Bruce Price for the Canadian Pacific Railway Company, opened in 1893. Built on the very site of Champlain's fort and Château Saint-Louis, it was expanded several times: the Citadel wing in 1899, the Mont-Carmel wing in 1908, the central tower in 1924. In 1943, Churchill and Roosevelt met here to plan the Normandy landings. Dufferin Terrace, a long wooden boardwalk alongside it, overlooks the St. Lawrence River from 60 meters up.
⏱️ 60 min
4
Québec's fortifications form 4.6 kilometers of ramparts with gates, cannons, and towers. Québec is the only walled city in North America north of Mexico whose walls remain intact. The first palisades date from Champlain in 1608. The current ramparts were built between 1693 and 1871, blending French (Vauban-style) and British influences. The Citadel, built between 1820 and 1850, is the largest British fortress in North America; it's still occupied by the Royal 22e Régiment, the Canadian Armed Forces' only francophone regiment.
⏱️ 75 min

Quebec

6 photos
Day 10
🔍

Discover

1
Montmorency Falls plunges 83 meters into the St. Lawrence River—30 meters taller than Niagara Falls. Fed by the Montmorency River cascading down the Laurentian escarpment, it bears the name of the Duke of Montmorency, viceroy of New France in the 17th century. A suspension bridge at the summit crosses the spray curtain. In winter, spray freezes at the falls' base to form an ice cone called "pain de sucre" (sugar loaf), which Québécois have been climbing since the 19th century.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Île d'Orléans, connected to the mainland by a single bridge since 1935, is a repository of rural Québec. Six parishes founded in the 17th century (the oldest, Sainte-Famille, dates from 1661) line a 67-kilometer road circling the island. Maple groves, apple orchards, strawberry fields, and vineyards compose an agricultural landscape barely changed since the French regime. The island counts over 600 heritage buildings, some from the 18th century.
⏱️ 120 min
3
Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupré Basilica, a pilgrimage site since 1658, draws hundreds of thousands of visitors annually. The current Romanesque Revival building dates from 1926—the two previous basilicas were destroyed by fire. Its facade is flanked by two 91-meter steeples. Inside, 240 stained-glass windows recount the life of Saint Anne. Crutches and prosthetics hanging at the entrance testify to healings pilgrims attribute to the saint.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Island tour by car or bicycle, with stops at farms to taste ice cider, artisan cheeses, and homemade preserves. Producers open their doors from May to October.
⏱️ 90 min

Baie Saint Paul

2 photos
Day 11
🔍

Discover

1
Route 362, between Beaupré and Baie-Saint-Paul, winds along the river with panoramas of the Charlevoix mountains and Île d'Orléans. Charlevoix has been a UNESCO World Biosphere Reserve since 1988, owing to its astrobleme—a 56-kilometer-diameter meteorite impact crater 400 million years old that shaped the entire region's geography.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Baie-Saint-Paul, nestled in a valley where the Gouffre River meets the St. Lawrence, is nicknamed the "Artists' Country." Painters like Clarence Gagnon, Marc-Aurèle Fortin, and Jean-Paul Lemieux set up their easels here in the early 20th century. Rue Saint-Jean-Baptiste concentrates Canada's highest density of art galleries. Cirque du Soleil was also born in Baie-Saint-Paul in 1984, emerging from a street performers' troupe led by a fire-breather from Québec City. The Museum of Contemporary Art has organized an international symposium every summer since 1982.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Isle-aux-Coudres, accessible by free ferry from Saint-Joseph-de-la-Rive (15 minutes), is an 11-kilometer-long island in the river. Jacques Cartier named it in 1535 for the abundant hazelnut trees (coudriers) he found. Cycling around the island covers roughly 23 kilometers on flat road. Two heritage-classified mills stand here: a water mill and a windmill, both built in the 19th century, which operated in tandem until 1948.
⏱️ 120 min
🎯

Activities

→
Bicycle tour of Isle-aux-Coudres: 23-kilometer loop on flat road, with stops at heritage mills, artisan shops, and river viewpoints. Bike rentals available near the dock.
⏱️ 120 min

La Malbaie

2 photos
Day 12
🔍

Discover

1
Hautes-Gorges-de-la-Rivière-Malbaie National Park protects deep valleys whose cliffs rank among the tallest east of the Canadian Rockies. The Malbaie River carved these spectacular gorges over millions of years. The name "Acropole des Draveurs" comes from a 1972 poem by Félix-Antoine Savard honoring the log drivers—workers who, each spring at snowmelt, cast felled logs into the river to float them downstream to mills.
⏱️ 30 min
🎯

Activities

→
Acropole des Draveurs hike: 10.8 kilometers round-trip, 800 meters of elevation gain, summit at 1,048 meters. The first two kilometers climb steeply on natural stone steps. Beyond, the maple forest gives way to alpine tundra. Three successive summits offer plunging views over the Malbaie River 800 meters below and the Charlevoix mountain ranges. At the third summit, view two glacial lakes. Bring warm clothing: it's 5 to 10°C cooler at the wind-exposed summit.
⏱️ 360 min
→
Alternative for those not attempting the big hike: rabaska (large canoe) or tour boat cruise on the Malbaie River at the gorge bottom. Upward view of vertiginous forest-covered walls. The Riverain trail (easy, 4 km) follows the river with cliff viewpoints.
⏱️ 120 min

Tadoussac

10 photos
Day 13
🔍

Discover

1
Drive to Tadoussac on Route 138 along the river. The road crosses Charlevoix villages (Saint-Siméon, Baie-des-Rochers) with glimpses of the ever-widening St. Lawrence. At Baie-Sainte-Catherine, a free ferry crosses the Saguenay River in 10 minutes—the only road access to Tadoussac. From the ferry deck, view the fjord mouth, less than a kilometer wide here. With luck, you'll spot belugas from the boat.
⏱️ 30 min
2
Tadoussac, a village of 800, occupies one of the world's most beautiful bays (member of the Club of the Most Beautiful Bays in the World since 1998). Jacques Cartier anchored here in 1535. In 1600, Pierre Chauvin founded New France's first fur trading post—eight years before Champlain founded Québec City. A reconstruction of the trading post, facing the beach, traces the history of exchanges with First Nations.
⏱️ 60 min
3
The Chapelle des Indiens (1747) is Canada's oldest surviving wooden chapel, with its red roof and white walls. Hôtel Tadoussac, recognizable by its large red roof, has welcomed visitors since 1864—the first tourists arrived by steamboat from Montréal and Québec. The Tadoussac Dunes, 5 kilometers south of the village, form a striking landscape of sand and forest bordering the river.
⏱️ 75 min
🎯

Activities

→
Observation cruise from Les Escoumins
⏱️ 60 min

Tadoussac

10 photos
Day 14
🔍

Discover

1
The Marine Mammal Interpretation Centre (CIMM), run by the Group for Research and Education on Marine Mammals (GREMM), presents the 13 cetacean species frequenting the St. Lawrence estuary. Life-size skeletons include a 13-meter sperm whale. Centre naturalists individually track humpback whales and belugas, identifying them by skin or fluke markings.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Saguenay Fjord National Park protects the shores of the world's southernmost glacial fjord. Carved by glaciers 10,000 years ago, the Saguenay reaches 276 meters depth at Cap Éternité but only 20 meters at its mouth—glacial debris having formed a natural sill. At Baie Éternité, the cliffs of Cap Trinité rise over 300 meters sheer above the fjord's black water. The Statue trail (3.5 km, 1h30) leads to a 9-meter Virgin Mary statue installed in 1881 atop the cliff.
⏱️ 120 min
🎯

Activities

→
Whale-watching cruise in the St. Lawrence estuary at the Saguenay confluence. The meeting of the Saguenay's cold, fresh water with the river's saltwater creates upwellings that bring krill and small fish to the surface, attracting cetaceans. From May to October, you can observe minke whales, fin whales (second-largest animal on Earth), humpback whales, and occasionally blue whales. St. Lawrence belugas, a resident population of roughly 1,700, frequent the area year-round.
⏱️ 150 min

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Peak tourist season, warmest weather, outdoor activities, highest prices, long daylight hours, festival season.

Fall (September-October): Spectacular autumn colors, comfortable temperatures, harvest season, fewer crowds, excellent photography.

Winter (December-March): Winter sports paradise, Northern Lights, Christmas markets, cold temperatures, budget accommodations, unique experiences.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather returning, fewer crowds, moderate prices, wildflower blooms, shoulder season advantages.

Regional Variations: West Coast mild year-round, Prairies continental extremes, Maritimes maritime climate, North extremely cold.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 11 and 17 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Canada?

Vast wilderness, multicultural cities, and natural wonders from Niagara Falls to Northern Lights. Experience French culture in Quebec, Rocky Mountain adventures, and friendly Canadian hospitality. The country offers incredible outdoor recreation and diverse cultural experiences across massive landscapes. Canada provides safety, beauty, and adventure in North America's northern giant.
