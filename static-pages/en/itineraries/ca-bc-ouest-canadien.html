Western Canada Road Trip: Rockies and Pacific Coast

Glaciers carve turquoise lakes at 1,700 metres elevation, the Icefields Parkway unfurls 230 km of snowy summits, temperate rainforest meets the Pacific Ocean. Western Canada, between Rockies and coast, is a three-week driving adventure.

📅 Best period June, July, August, September

From Vancouver on the Pacific to the turquoise lakes of Lake Louise and Moraine, from the peaks of Banff and Jasper to the vineyards of the Okanagan, from Tofino's ancient rainforest to Victoria's colonial charm—Western Canada condenses mountains, glaciers, forests, and ocean into a single journey.

  • Lake Louise: turquoise lake at 1,731 m elevation, fed by the Victoria Glacier
  • Banff: Canada's first national park, created in 1885 around hot springs
  • Icefields Parkway: 230 km of highway through glaciers, lakes, and peaks over 3,000 m
  • Tofino: 16 km of wild beach and ancient rainforest on the Pacific coast
  • Helmcken Falls: Canada's fourth-highest waterfall, 141 m freefall

Vancouver

9 photos
Day 1
🔍

Discover

1
Gastown, Vancouver's oldest neighbourhood, takes its name from "Gassy" Jack Deighton, a former steamboat captain born in Hull, Yorkshire, who arrived in September 1867 with just six dollars in his pocket, his Indigenous wife, and a yellow dog. Legend has it he promised sawmill workers all the whisky they could drink in exchange for their help—24 hours later, a saloon stood complete. The district, devastated by the Great Fire of 1886, was rebuilt in brick and stone. Designated a National Historic Site in 2009, it retains its original cobblestones and Victorian facades. At the corner of Water and Cambie Streets, the 1977 steam clock (designed by horologist Raymond Saunders) runs partly on steam from the city's underground district heating system and partly on electric motors: every quarter-hour, its brass whistles play the Westminster Chimes, and a plume of steam shoots from the top on the hour.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Stanley Park covers 405 hectares of temperate rainforest on a peninsula—nearly 20% larger than New York's Central Park. Opened September 27, 1888, and named after Governor General Frederick Stanley, it was designated a National Historic Site in 1988. The seawall, an 8.8-km promenade, circles the park along the ocean. At Brockton Point, nine totem poles carved by First Nations artists (Squamish, Haida, Kwakwaka'wakw) stand in a clearing—the collection began in the 1920s when the Parks Board acquired four totems from Alert Bay. The current poles, moved to Brockton Point in 1962, are mostly red cedar replicas of originals preserved in museums.
⏱️ 90 min
3
English Bay and Sunset Beach complete the park's western loop along Vancouver's most popular urban beaches. English Bay Beach, facing due west, offers Pacific sunsets with the mountains of Vancouver Island as backdrop.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Walk or cycle the Stanley Park seawall, a flat 8.8-km loop passing Prospect Point (with views of Lions Gate Bridge), Siwash Rock, and Third Beach.
⏱️ 90 min

Vancouver

9 photos
Day 2
🔍

Discover

1
Granville Island occupies a 14-hectare artificial peninsula created in 1915 by dredging False Creek to accommodate heavy industry. Abandoned in the 1970s, the site was rehabilitated and the Public Market opened July 12, 1979, with 21 vendors. The painted corrugated-metal buildings preserve their original beams and a ten-tonne hoist hanging from the ceiling, remnants of the former cable factory. Today the market hosts about fifty fresh-produce merchants.
⏱️ 90 min
2
The Kitsilano neighbourhood, once a 1960s counterculture stronghold, stretches between Kits Beach and streets lined with bungalows. Kitsilano Pool, 137 metres long, is Canada's largest saltwater outdoor pool.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Cross the Capilano Suspension Bridge in North Vancouver, a 137-metre footbridge suspended 70 metres above the Capilano River. Originally built in 1889 with hemp ropes and cedar planks by Scottish real-estate developer George Grant Mackay, it has been rebuilt four times since. The park also features the Treetops Adventure (seven suspension bridges in the canopy 33 metres above ground) and the Cliffwalk (a 213-metre cantilevered walkway bolted to the granite cliff face). Free alternative: Lynn Canyon Suspension Bridge, lower but set in pristine nature.
⏱️ 150 min

Kelowna

5 photos
Day 3
🔍

Discover

1
Stop in Hope, a small town at the foot of the Cascades where the Fraser Valley narrows. Six kilometres from downtown, the Othello Tunnels (Coquihalla Canyon Provincial Park) are five railway tunnels carved through granite between 1911 and 1916 for the Kettle Valley Railway. Abandoned since 1959, the pedestrian path traverses the tunnels and crosses the narrow Coquihalla River gorge on old railway bridges—a spectacular passage between vertical rock walls.
⏱️ 45 min
2
The route then takes the Coquihalla Highway (Route 5), built in 1986, which crosses a pass at 1,244 metres, before turning onto Route 97C through the high plateaus. Arrival in Kelowna reveals Okanagan Lake, 135 km long and 232 metres deep. According to the oral tradition of the Syilx First Nations, the lake harbours N'ha-a-itk (Ogopogo), a lake serpent.
⏱️ 30 min
3
Stroll through Kelowna's Waterfront Park and City Park, landscaped along the lake's eastern shore. City Park features a large sandy beach and an unobstructed view of the William R. Bennett floating bridge (694 metres, opened 2008) linking Kelowna to West Kelowna.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

Hike in Knox Mountain Park for views over Okanagan Lake

Kelowna

5 photos
Day 4
🔍

Discover

1
Knox Mountain Park overlooks Kelowna from its northern shore. The trail climbs 2 km to the lookout where the panorama embraces Okanagan Lake, the terraced orchards, and semi-arid hills carpeted with sagebrush—the valley receives less than 400 mm of rain annually.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Hot Sands Beach, on the eastern shore, is Kelowna's most popular beach. The lake water, of glacial origin, warms sufficiently in summer for swimming thanks to the shallow depth near shore.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Wine tasting in the Okanagan Valley, British Columbia's largest wine region with over 200 estates across 250 km. Viticulture dates to the 1850s when missionaries planted vines for sacramental wine. After near-disappearance during Prohibition, revival came only in the 1930s with Calona Vineyards, the province's first commercial winery. Noble grape varieties (Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Riesling) replaced hybrids in the 1970s-80s. The continental microclimate—14 hours of daily sunshine in summer—produces award-winning wines, notably icewines.
⏱️ 150 min

Revelstoke

7 photos
Day 5
🔍

Discover

1
The Trans-Canada Highway runs east through Vernon and Eagle Pass. At Craigellachie, a monument commemorates the Last Spike of the Canadian Pacific Railway, driven on November 7, 1885, at 9:22 a.m. by financier Donald Smith. This act sealed the transcontinental railway link of 5,000 km connecting Montreal to the Pacific—a promise made to British Columbia to join Confederation in 1871. Smith missed his first swing and bent the spike; he had it straightened and cut into jewellery for the wives of the dignitaries present.
⏱️ 30 min
2
Arrival in Revelstoke, a small mountain town nestled at the confluence of the Columbia and Illecillewaet Rivers, between the Selkirk and Monashee Mountains. The historic downtown preserves early-20th-century facades from the era when Revelstoke served as a dispatching centre for the Canadian Pacific.
⏱️ 45 min

Revelstoke

7 photos
Day 6
🔍

Discover

1
Mount Revelstoke National Park, created in 1914 at the initiative of the residents themselves, is the only Canadian national park whose summit you can reach by car. The Meadows in the Sky Parkway, a 26-km road built between 1911 and 1927, climbs 1,400 metres in elevation—from temperate rainforest cedars to alpine meadows ablaze with wildflowers in August. At the summit (1,930 m), views sweep across the Monashee and Selkirk ranges.
⏱️ 120 min
2
The Giant Cedars Boardwalk, a 500-metre walkway along the Trans-Canada Highway, traverses an inland temperate rainforest of red cedars and hemlocks, some exceeding 800 years old.
⏱️ 45 min
3
Glacier National Park (1,349 km², created in 1886) protects Rogers Pass, at 1,330 m elevation, discovered in 1881 by Major Albert Bowman Rogers for the Canadian Pacific. The pass receives an average of 14 metres of snow per year. Avalanches killed 200 workers in 30 years, forcing the excavation of the Connaught Tunnel (8 km) in 1916.
⏱️ 90 min

Lake Louise

9 photos
Day 7
🔍

Discover

1
Drive via Golden and Kicking Horse Pass to Banff National Park. Lake Louise, at 1,731 metres elevation, stretches 2.4 km long and reaches 70 metres deep. Its turquoise colour comes from rock flour—particles ground by the Victoria Glacier. The Stoney-Nakoda called it Ho-run-num-nay, "the lake of little fishes." In 1882, Thomas Wilson, a guide and packer working for the Canadian Pacific, became the first non-Indigenous person to see it.
⏱️ 60 min
2
The Fairmont Château Lake Louise, whose construction dates to 1890, occupies the northern shore facing the Victoria Glacier. The lakeshore trail (3.4 km, flat) follows the shore to the western end. The lake, named in honour of Princess Louise Caroline Alberta, daughter of Queen Victoria, gave its name to the province.
⏱️ 75 min

Banff

7 photos
Day 9
🔍

Discover

1
The town of Banff grew around hot springs discovered in November 1883 by three Canadian Pacific Railway workers—William and Tom McCardell and Frank McCabe—who spotted a steaming hole on the slopes of Sulphur Mountain. William descended into the cavity using a ladder hewn from a tree trunk and discovered a pool of hot water beneath stalactites. This discovery led to the creation in 1885 of the Banff Hot Springs Reserve, the nucleus of Canada's first national park.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Ride the Banff Gondola to the summit of Sulphur Mountain at 2,281 metres. The 360-degree panorama embraces the Bow Valley, Mount Rundle, Cascade Mountain, and the snow-clad ranges of the Rockies. A wooden boardwalk leads to the summit of Sanson Peak, where Norman Sanson, a volunteer meteorologist, climbed over 1,000 times between 1903 and 1945 to check his instruments.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Hike through Johnston Canyon: the trail follows metal catwalks bolted to the canyon wall above Johnston Creek. The Lower Falls are accessible after 1.1 km; the Upper Falls (30 metres high) after 2.7 km. Option to continue to the Ink Pots, five coloured mineral springs in an alpine meadow (5.8 km one way).
⏱️ 150 min

Banff

7 photos
Day 10
🔍

Discover

1
Bow Falls, ten minutes on foot from downtown, marks a dramatic drop of the Bow River at the foot of the Fairmont Banff Springs Hotel (built in 1888 by architect Bruce Price in a "château" style inspired by Scottish and French castles). Surprise Corner, an overlook viewpoint, offers Banff's most photographed vista: the falls in the foreground with the château behind.
⏱️ 45 min
2
Lake Minnewanka, 21 km long, is the largest lake in Banff National Park. Its name, in the Stoney language, means "lake of the spirits." A village submerged by rising water during dam construction in 1941 rests several metres beneath the surface.
⏱️ 60 min
3
Tunnel Mountain Drive offers panoramas of the Bow Valley and the hoodoos—columns of sedimentary rock sculpted by erosion, 3 to 7 metres high, capped with more resistant stone blocks.
⏱️ 30 min
🎯

Activities

→
Soak at Banff Upper Hot Springs, an outdoor hot spring at 1,585 metres elevation. Water emerges at 47°C and is cooled to about 39°C in the pool. Step through the changing-room doors and into the pool facing Mount Rundle—it's one of the rare places in the world where you bathe in a natural hot spring with views of 3,000-metre peaks.
⏱️ 90 min

Jasper

9 photos
Day 11
🔍

Discover

1
The Icefields Parkway, 230 km between Lake Louise and Jasper, traverses the heart of the Rockies. Stop at Bow Lake, a turquoise lake at the foot of Crowfoot Glacier, then climb to Bow Summit viewpoint (2,069 m, the highest point on the highway) to gaze upon Peyto Lake, recognizable by its fox shape and intense blue.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Stop at Mistaya Canyon, a narrow gorge where the Mistaya River plunges into a limestone defile polished by water. The footbridge above the canyon lets you see the potholes carved by whirlpools.
⏱️ 30 min
3
The Columbia Icefield, 325 km² in area and up to 365 metres thick, is the largest icefield in the Rockies. Discovered in 1898 by explorer J. Norman Collie from the summit of Mount Athabasca, it feeds three oceans (Pacific, Atlantic, and Arctic) through its meltwater—a unique case of triple continental divide. The Athabasca Glacier, one of its six main glaciers, has retreated 1.5 km in 125 years.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Jasper SkyTram for panoramas of the Rockies
⏱️ 60 min

Jasper

9 photos
Day 12
🔍

Discover

1
Athabasca Falls, 30 km south of Jasper, are not the highest (23 metres) but rank among the most powerful in the Rockies. The Athabasca River plunges into a quartzite gorge it has carved since the end of the last glaciation. Marked trails and viewing platforms allow you to observe the water's power from several angles.
⏱️ 45 min
2
Maligne Canyon, a 50-metre-deep gorge cut through limestone by the Maligne River, is explored via a network of six numbered bridges. Between bridges 1 and 2, the walls are separated by only a few metres while the river roars below.
⏱️ 60 min
🎯

Activities

→
Cruise on Maligne Lake to Spirit Island. Twenty-two kilometres long, Maligne is the largest natural lake in the Canadian Rockies. Spirit Island, a tiny wooded islet surrounded by turquoise water and snowy peaks, became one of Canada's most reproduced images after appearing in a calendar series in the 1950s. The cruise lasts about 90 minutes round trip.
⏱️ 120 min

Pyramid Lake and Pyramid Island, connected to shore by a small wooden bridge, form one of Jasper's most peaceful landscapes. The glacial lake reflects Pyramid Mountain (2,763 m), from which it takes its name.
⏱️ 60 min

Hike the Valley of the Five Lakes, an easy loop traversing five small lakes in shades from jade to deep blue, each coloured differently according to its rocky bottom composition and light.

Ride the Jasper SkyTram to the summit of Whistlers Mountain at 2,277 metres. It's Canada's longest and highest aerial tramway. From the summit, on clear days, the view extends to Mount Robson (3,954 m, highest peak in the Canadian Rockies), the Athabasca Valley, and the mountain ranges beyond.

Leaving Jasper westward on the Yellowhead Highway (Route 16), Mount Robson appears head-on—at 3,954 metres, it's the highest peak in the Canadian Rockies. A viewpoint and visitor centre at the mountain's foot allow you to contemplate its north face, often veiled in clouds. Only about one in two climbers reaches the summit, such are the capricious weather conditions.

The road continues through Yellowhead Pass (1,131 m) then turns onto Highway 5 south. It passes Blue River and follows the North Thompson River, bordered by forests of spruce and aspen, to Clearwater, gateway to Wells Gray Provincial Park.

Discover

1
Helmcken Falls, in the heart of Wells Gray Provincial Park, plunge 141 metres into a volcanic canyon—Canada's fourth-highest waterfall. Protecting these falls was one of the reasons for creating the park in 1939. Discovered in 1913 by surveyor Robert Lee, they were named in honour of Dr. John Sebastian Helmcken, a Hudson's Bay Company physician who helped bring British Columbia into Confederation in 1871. In winter, spray forms a 50-metre-high ice cone at the base of the falls.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Spahats Creek Falls (75 metres), accessible from a viewpoint just 10 km from the park entrance, tumble into a volcanic gorge with columnar basalt walls. "Spahats" means "bear" in the local First Nations language.
⏱️ 30 min
3
Dawson Falls, nicknamed "Mini Niagara," form a 90-metre-wide curtain of water cascading in steps over 200,000-year-old lava flows. A ten-minute walk through old-growth forest leads to the banks of the Murtle River.
⏱️ 45 min
🎯

Activities

→
Canoe or kayak on Clearwater Lake, 25 km long, formed 6,000 years ago when a lava flow from Kostal Volcano dammed the Clearwater River. The water is remarkably clear—visibility reaches several metres.
⏱️ 120 min

Tofino

8 photos
Day 19
🔍

Discover

1
Pacific Rim National Park protects Long Beach, 16 km of grey sand pounded by Pacific waves, backed by temperate rainforest. The spray-laden air, beached tree trunks, and patrolling bald eagles compose an intact wilderness landscape.
⏱️ 75 min
2
Meares Island, accessible by water taxi from Tofino (10 minutes), harbours the Big Tree Trail, a boardwalk path winding between red cedars over 1,000 years old, some reaching 4 metres in diameter. The island has been protected since 1984 thanks to mobilization by the Tla-o-qui-aht Nation, which opposed logging by declaring the island a tribal park—one of the first victories of the First Nations environmental movement in British Columbia.
⏱️ 90 min
🎯

Activities

→
Surf or watch the waves at Cox Bay or Chesterman Beach. Tofino is Canada's surf capital thanks to its consistent waves and sandy bottom. In autumn and winter, Pacific storms generate spectacular swells—a local tradition is "storm watching" from the beaches or accommodation terraces.

Victoria

6 photos
Day 20
🔍

Discover

1
Return drive to Vancouver Island's east coast (about 4 hours). Victoria's Inner Harbour is dominated by the British Columbia Parliament Buildings, completed in 1898 by architect Francis Rattenbury in a neo-Baroque style. The edifice, whose facade measures 150 metres, is illuminated by over 3,000 lights at night. Facing the harbour, the same Rattenbury designed the Empress Hotel (1908), a grand château-style hotel that hosts a traditional afternoon tea.
⏱️ 60 min
2
Craigdarroch Castle, a 39-room Victorian mansion built between 1887 and 1890 for Robert Dunsmuir, a coal baron who was then British Columbia's wealthiest man. He died before construction was completed. The interior preserves its oak panelling, original stained glass, and an 87-step staircase leading to the tower from which views extend to the Olympic Mountains of Washington State.
⏱️ 75 min
3
Beacon Hill Park, 75 hectares of gardens and meadows facing the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Here stands the Mile Zero marker of the Trans-Canada Highway—the 7,821-km highway linking Victoria to St. John's, Newfoundland.
⏱️ 45 min

❓ Frequently asked questions

What weather should you expect?

Summer (June-August): Peak tourist season, warmest weather, outdoor activities, highest prices, long daylight hours, festival season.

Fall (September-October): Spectacular autumn colors, comfortable temperatures, harvest season, fewer crowds, excellent photography.

Winter (December-March): Winter sports paradise, Northern Lights, Christmas markets, cold temperatures, budget accommodations, unique experiences.

Spring (April-May): Mild weather returning, fewer crowds, moderate prices, wildflower blooms, shoulder season advantages.

Regional Variations: West Coast mild year-round, Prairies continental extremes, Maritimes maritime climate, North extremely cold.

How many days should I plan?

We believe you will enjoy this itinerary best by dedicating between 17 and 25 days. The next step will allow you to adjust the duration of your stay.

What to discover in Canada?

Vast wilderness, multicultural cities, and natural wonders from Niagara Falls to Northern Lights. Experience French culture in Quebec, Rocky Mountain adventures, and friendly Canadian hospitality. The country offers incredible outdoor recreation and diverse cultural experiences across massive landscapes. Canada provides safety, beauty, and adventure in North America's northern giant.
