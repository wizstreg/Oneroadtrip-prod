Gard Road Trip: Roman Nîmes, Pont du Gard & Camargue

Where ancient arenas rise toward Mediterranean skies, where the Pont du Gard spans the river in timeless majesty, and where pink flamingos mirror themselves in crystalline salt waters beneath the Camargue light.

Discover the treasures of Gard between exceptional Roman ruins, Cévennes mountains, and Camargue wetlands. From Nîmes and its arena to the UNESCO Pont du Gard, from medieval Uzès to Anduze's bamboo groves, from Aigues-Mortes ramparts to pink salt marshes, explore a region of endless contrasts.

  • Nîmes: UNESCO amphitheater and Maison Carrée
  • Pont du Gard: monumental Roman aqueduct
  • Uzès: France's first duchy
  • Bambouseraie d'Anduze: giant bamboo forest
  • Cévennes: chestnut groves and authentic villages
  • Aigues-Mortes: medieval fortified town
  • Camargue salt marshes: flamingos and salt
  • Plage de l'Espiguette: wild dunes

Discover

1
Arènes de Nîmes, one of the world's best-preserved Roman amphitheaters at 433 feet long and 69 feet high. Built in the 1st century AD, it held 24,000 spectators who came to watch gladiatorial combat. The two-tiered exterior arcade in honey-colored Vers-Pont-du-Gard stone demonstrates Roman engineering mastery. The free audioguide in seven languages recreates the atmosphere of ancient games. Today the monument hosts bullfights, concerts, and historical reenactments including the Roman Days of Nîmes each late April.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Maison Carrée, a 1st-century BC Roman temple inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2023. The Corinthian facade with 30 fluted columns illustrates the harmony of classical proportions. It's the only completely preserved ancient temple from the entire Roman world. Innovative museography with immersive projection "Nemausus, the Birth of Nîmes" traces 2,000 years of history on the interior walls. The building successively served as a consular house, church, prefecture, and departmental archives.
⏱️ 75 min
3
Tour Magne, remnant of Roman ramparts dominating Nîmes from atop Mont Cavalier at 105 feet high. Built under Augustus, it marked the northwestern corner of fortified walls that stretched nearly four miles. Climbing the 140 steps leads to a panoramic platform offering 360° views over the city, garrigue scrubland, and on clear days, Mont Ventoux and the Cévennes mountains. The octagonal tower integrated into the most imposing defensive system in Roman Gaul.
⏱️ 60 min
4
Jardins de la Fontaine, an 18th-century public park designed around the sacred spring that gave birth to Nemausus. Majestic tree-lined pathways bordered by balustrades and neoclassical statues follow a monumental perspective inspired by Versailles. The Temple of Diana, a mysterious Roman sanctuary with collapsed arcades, stands at the garden's heart. Terraced pools capture spring waters while staircases climb toward Mont Cavalier and Tour Magne. In summer, the garden becomes a cool refuge beneath centuries-old plane trees.
⏱️ 90 min

Activities

→
Experience the Nîmes Férias, major bullfighting events in May (Pentecost) and September. For five days, corridas in the arena, lively bodegas, musical peñas, free concerts, and marching bands invade downtown. Aficionados in white outfits stroll from bar to bar in a festive, convivial atmosphere. Abrivados and bandidos see Camargue bulls running through the streets. Goyesque corridas feature bullfighters in period costumes. The electric atmosphere celebrates Camargue and Spanish bullfighting culture.
⏱️ 240 min💡 Very festive atmosphere, expect major crowds
→
Taste brandade de Nîmes at a traditional restaurant in the historic center. This specialty made from desalted cod, milk, olive oil, and garlic is served hot with croutons. Family establishments perpetuate ancestral recipes passed down since the 18th century. Brandade houses also offer versions with truffles or gratinéed. This emblematic Nîmes dish pairs with white Costières wine or chilled rosé.
⏱️ 90 min
→
Wander through Nîmes' Provençal markets, particularly the one on Boulevard Jean-Jaurès Monday and Friday mornings. Stalls overflow with Gard fruits and vegetables, Pélardon AOC goat cheese, artisanal charcuterie, honey, seasonal fruits. Local producers sell garrigue honey, homemade jams, aromatic plants. The lively atmosphere resonates with southern accents and spirited haggling. Aromas of rotisseries, socca, and paella float between the aisles.

Discover

1
Pont du Gard, masterpiece of Roman engineering inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1985. The three-tiered aqueduct bridge rises 161 feet high and stretches 902 feet across the Gardon River. Built in the 1st century to carry water from Uzès to Nîmes over 31 miles, it illustrates ancient hydraulic mastery with a gradient of only 40 feet. The 52 superimposed arches use limestone blocks weighing up to six tons assembled without mortar. The 408-acre preserved site offers a museum, garrigue trails, Gardon beaches, and summer sound and light shows.
⏱️ 180 min
2
Mémoires de Garrigue trail around Pont du Gard, a 0.9-mile loop through Mediterranean landscape. The path discovers Roman aqueduct remnants punctuating holm oaks, Aleppo pines, vineyards, and olive groves. Educational panels explain underground canal construction, Roman techniques, and garrigue ecosystem. Aromatic plants—thyme, rosemary, rockrose—perfume the stony path. Rocky outcrops reveal urgonian limestone quarried by ancient builders. The shaded walk invites contemplation of the monument from different viewpoints.
⏱️ 75 min
3
Duché d'Uzès, France's first duchy created in 1565. The ducal palace's Renaissance facade overlooks Place aux Herbes bordered by medieval arcades. The 11th-century Tour Bermonde, a rectangular keep, stands beside the Gothic Saint-Hubert chapel. Historic apartments reveal period furniture, Flemish tapestries, and family memorabilia of the Dukes of Uzès. 14th-century vaulted cellars host temporary exhibitions. The reconstructed medieval garden cultivates medicinal and aromatic plants. The private residence still inhabited by the ducal family offers guided audio tours.
⏱️ 90 min
4
Tour Fenestrelle in Uzès, France's only circular bell tower dating from the 12th century. The 138-foot cylindrical tower features three stories of twin bays opening to the four cardinal points. The exceptional Romanesque architecture served as a bell tower for the medieval cathedral destroyed during the Wars of Religion. Climbing 135 spiral staircase steps leads to a panoramic platform embracing the Eure valley, garrigue, and Cévennes. The bells once rhythmed religious life and agricultural activities of the town.
⏱️ 60 min

Activities

→
Swimming in the Gardon River beneath the Pont du Gard's arches. Developed beaches 650 feet from the monument offer supervised swimming areas in summer with play areas for children. The river's clear, cool waters invite refreshing dips after visiting the site. Shaded banks provide picnic areas under tamarisk trees. Swimming facing the ancient bridge creates a unique experience blending heritage and nature. The moderate current allows floating on tubes and family water games.
⏱️ 120 min💡 Arrive early in high season, limited spaces
→
Uzès truffle market from November to March, meeting place for truffle hunters and dealers. Every Saturday morning on Place aux Herbes, the black diamond market gathers local producers selling fresh tubers weighed discreetly. The hushed transaction atmosphere contrasts with the square's usual liveliness. Gard truffles, melanosporum variety, rival those from Périgord and Vaucluse. Restaurants offer truffle menus celebrating the precious fungus. The Maison de la Truffe organizes discovery workshops and truffle-hunting demonstrations.
⏱️ 90 min💡 Traditional bargaining, variable quality
→
Visit potter workshops perpetuating Uzès ceramic tradition since antiquity. Artisans shape utilitarian and decorative pieces on the wheel in characteristic red clay. Ancestral techniques coexist with contemporary creations: Provençal jars, glazed tableware, sculptures. Demonstrations reveal precise gestures of throwing, glazing, and traditional kiln firing. Workshop-boutiques dot historic center streets offering original local production. Poterie du Mas de Rouvière perpetuates family know-how for five generations.
⏱️ 75 min

Discover

1
Bambouseraie en Cévennes in Générargues, a unique botanical garden in Europe created in 1856. The giant bamboo forest covers 84 acres sheltering over 1,000 bamboo varieties reaching 82 feet high. The Zen Japanese garden with red bridge, stone lanterns, and koi carp offers a meditative interlude. The reconstructed Laotian village presents traditional stilt houses surrounded by banana trees and lotus flowers. Tropical greenhouses gather tree ferns, orchids, and exotic plants. The maze-like park invites shaded strolling, refreshing in summer.
⏱️ 150 min
2
Village of Anduze, traditional Cévennes town and gateway to the Cévennes at the Gardon confluence. Tortuous medieval alleyways climb from quays bordering the river. The Protestant temple clock tower overlooks a covered square hosting weekly markets. Corbelled houses with colorful facades testify to traditional Cévennes architecture. Workshops perpetuate manufacture of famous Anduze vases, monumental glazed pottery adorning gardens since the 17th century. Shops offer chestnuts, Pélardon, chestnut honey, and local products.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Maison Rouge – Musée des Vallées Cévenoles, installed in a 17th-century mansion. Ethnographic collections reconstruct daily Cévennes life: traditional interiors, agricultural tools, silk weaving looms, domestic utensils. The exhibition evokes the silk-farming epic with silkworm breeding that enriched the region until the 19th century. Thematic rooms present chestnut groves, crafts, Cévennes Protestantism, Wars of Religion. Temporary exhibitions deepen local history and heritage.
⏱️ 75 min
4
Steam Train of the Cévennes, historic tourist line connecting Anduze to Saint-Jean-du-Gard. Century-old locomotives pull Belle Époque carriages for eight miles along the Gardon de Mialet. The winding route crosses tunnels, viaducts, and spectacular Cévennes landscapes. Commentary relates the railway's history, created in 1880 to transport silk, wood, and chestnuts. Restored stations preserve period railway architecture. The one-hour round trip plunges into retro railway atmosphere.
⏱️ 120 min
🎯

Activities

→
Canoe-kayak descent of the Gardon River from Anduze. The clear river winds between limestone cliffs and pebble beaches under forest cover. Moderate rapids alternate with calm sections suitable for swimming. Rental companies offer routes from 4 to 15 miles depending on level and available time. The Gardon canyon reveals caves, emerald pools, and preserved wild landscapes. Cliffs shelter reintroduced griffon vultures soaring over gorges. The family activity allows discovering the Cévennes from the river.
⏱️ 180 min
→
Explore Trabuc Cave, an underground network of five miles with 0.75 miles developed for visits. Galleries reveal millennial formations: stalactites, stalagmites, ochre-tinted calcite draperies. The Lake of One Hundred Thousand Soldiers astounds with its 100,000 threadlike stalagmites unique in the world. Cathedral-like chambers impress with their dimensions and exceptional geological formations. The constant 57°F temperature offers welcome coolness in summer. The 45-minute guided tours recount the cave's use as a Camisard refuge.
⏱️ 90 min💡 Avoid if claustrophobic
→
Taste Cévennes products at local farms and producers. Pélardon AOC, small aged goat cheese, comes plain, ash-covered, or with aromatic herbs. Dried, creamed, or candied chestnuts illustrate the historic importance of the chestnut tree, the Cévennes "bread tree." Full-bodied chestnut honey, heather, or wildflower comes from transhumant apiaries. Sweet onions of the Cévennes AOC cook as confit or raw in salads. Artisan canneries offer terrines, confits, and traditional Cévennes dishes.

Village of Saint-Jean-du-Gard, typical Cévennes town in the heart of Cévennes National Park. Schist houses with slate roofs tier along the Gardon. The 12th-century Romanesque church stands beside a Protestant temple testifying to Cévennes Huguenot history. Paved streets lined with artisan workshops wind between fountains and shaded squares. Tuesday morning market animates the center with local products and friendly atmosphere. The multi-arched medieval bridge spans the Gardon offering views of surrounding mountains.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Musée des Vallées Cévenoles – Maison Rouge, ethnographic collection tracing traditional lifestyles. Reconstructions present authentic Cévennes interiors: rustic furniture, daily utensils, regional costumes. The silk-farming department evokes the epic of magnaneries and flourishing silk industry until 1900. Thematic rooms document terraced agriculture, sheep farming, century-old chestnut groves. The Camisard space relates Wars of Religion and Protestant resistance in the 18th century. Temporary exhibitions deepen intangible Cévennes heritage.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Atlantide Parc, family water park and leisure center on 12 acres. Play pools, water slides, and lazy river refresh during hot summer days. The wellness area offers spa, hammam, and jacuzzis in green surroundings. Land activities include mini-golf, trampolines, inflatable structures for children. Shaded picnic areas under plane trees allow family lunches. The park blends aquatic relaxation and entertainment in preserved natural environment.
⏱️ 180 min

Activities

→
Hike the Stevenson Trail (GR70) crossing the Cévennes. The Scottish writer covered 137 miles in 1878 with his donkey Modestine, recounted in "Travels with a Donkey in the Cévennes." The mythical trail winds through chestnut groves, schist ridges, deep valleys, and isolated hamlets. Stages around Saint-Jean-du-Gard discover typical Cévennes landscapes: cultivated terraces, pastoral drailles, mountain panoramas. Gîtes d'étape dot the route welcoming hikers. Donkey rental perpetuates the Stevensonian tradition.
⏱️ 240 min💡 Book accommodations in advance
→
Discover Cévennes chestnut groves, cultivated forests shaping terraced landscapes. The chestnut tree, the "bread tree," ensured Cévennes subsistence providing flour, building wood, and heating. Century-old orchards with gnarled trunks tier on slopes arranged in terraces supported by dry-stone walls. Autumn sees fallen chestnuts gathered and transformed into cream, jam, flour. Clèdes, vaulted buildings for drying, dot the chestnut groves. Interpretive trails explain this heritage silviculture inscribed by UNESCO.
⏱️ 120 min
→
Visit a traditional magnanerie, silkworm breeding building. Restored magnaneries testify to prosperous silk-farming industry in the 18th-19th centuries. Vaulted rooms sheltered racks supporting millions of worms devouring mulberry leaves. The complete process—from egg to cocoon, silk reeling, weaving—unfolds through exhibitions and demonstrations. Mulberry trees planted on terraces fed seasonal breeding operations. The Cévennes silk epic exported to Lyon enriched the region before declining against Asian competition.
⏱️ 90 min

Discover

1
Mine-témoin d'Alès, preserved historic mining site testifying to the Cévennes coal epic. Underground gallery descent recreates 19th-20th century miners' working conditions. Underground workings reveal extraction techniques, tools, wagons, and timbering. Museography traces industrial history of the Alès coal basin that employed up to 15,000 people. Collections display mine lamps, measuring instruments, installation models. The metal headframe dominates the site classified as Historic Monument. One-hour guided tours recount the dangerous daily life of the coal miners.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Saint-Jean-Baptiste Cathedral of Alès, 19th-century neoclassical building in the rebuilt city center. The monumental facade with Corinthian columns draws inspiration from Rome's Pantheon. The sober interior lit by a dome shelters a majestic organ and religious paintings. The cathedral quarter preserves Haussmannian buildings testifying to industrial urbanism. Place Gabriel-Péri bordered by cafés animates downtown. The neighboring Protestant temple recalls Protestantism's importance in Cévennes territory.
⏱️ 60 min
3
Musée-bibliothèque Pierre-André Benoît, modern and contemporary art collection installed in the former bishop's palace. The collection gathers 5,000 works by 20th-century creators: Picasso, Miró, Braque, Giacometti, Dubuffet. Artists' books, prints, drawings illustrate dialogue between poets and painters. Temporary exhibitions explore avant-garde artistic movements. The sculpted Mediterranean garden offers a plant setting for works. Free admission democratizes access to art.
⏱️ 75 min
4
Fort Vauban of Alès, 17th-century citadel dominating the city from Saint-Julien hill. The bastioned fortification designed by Vauban surveilled the Protestant region after Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. Pentagonal ramparts and casemates testify to classical military architecture. The panoramic site embraces the Alès agglomeration, Cévennes plain, and first mountain foothills. Ramparts now host walks and cultural events. Free access allows discovering fortifications and belvedere.
⏱️ 60 min

Alès market at Place de la Mairie, daily gathering under Belle Époque metal halls. Cévennes producers offer organic vegetables, goat cheeses, charcuterie, honey, seasonal fruits. Colorful stalls overflow with olives, tapenades, flavored oils, Cévennes wines. The southern atmosphere resonates with singing accents and animated conversations. Rotisseries emit appetizing aromas of roasted chickens and paella. The particularly abundant Saturday morning market attracts residents throughout the agglomeration.

Evening at Le Cratère, Alès national performing arts center programming quality shows. The cultural center offers contemporary theater, dance, concerts, comedy, circus in a 500-seat modular hall. Eclectic programming mixes nationally significant productions and emerging artists. Artist meetings, workshops, and residencies enrich cultural offerings. The café-restaurant welcomes audiences before and after performances. Box office allows reserving seats for varied artistic evenings.

The flat route accessible to all offers green escape near downtown.

Discover

1
1970s architecture of La Grande-Motte, visionary seaside resort created from scratch by Jean Balladur. Residential pyramids with staggered white facades draw inspiration from pre-Columbian and modern architecture. The Grande Pyramide, iconic 14-story building, dominates the seafront. Seaside residences with bold geometric forms—Point Zéro, Grande Motte, Acapulco—illustrate the seaside utopia of the post-war boom years. The 20th-century architectural heritage classified with Heritage of the 20th Century label attracts modern architecture enthusiasts. Green spaces integrated into buildings create a unique garden city on the coast.
⏱️ 90 min
2
Port de plaisance of La Grande-Motte, modern marina with 1,600 berths nestled between dunes and pyramids. Pontoons welcome yachts, sailboats, and pleasure boats in a protected basin. Lively quays border restaurants, ice cream shops, and nautical boutiques in relaxed seaside atmosphere. Sailing school offers introductions and courses for all levels. Developed promenades invite strolling between boats and brutalist architecture. Sunsets set ablaze white pyramid facades reflecting in calm waters.

Beaches of La Grande-Motte, three miles of supervised fine sand bordered by protected dunes. Grande Plage facing the pyramids offers lifeguard stations, nautical clubs, and deckchair rental. Themed beaches—Plage du Couchant, de l'Esplanade—adapt to different practices: family swimming, water sports, sunbathing. Breakwaters create calm swimming zones for children. Handicapped access with tiralos allows swimming for all. Beaches with Blue Flag label guarantee water quality and preserved environment.

Discover

1
Ramparts of Aigues-Mortes, perfectly preserved 13th-century medieval fortified walls. The mile-long crenellated walls punctuated by 20 towers completely encircle the town. The walkway accessible along the entire perimeter offers panoramas over Camargue, pink salt marshes, and Mediterranean Sea. Military architecture designed under Louis IX (Saint Louis) illustrates medieval defensive art with loopholes, hoardings, and machicolations. Fortified gates—Porte de la Gardette, Porte de l'Organeau—controlled access to the port town. Sunset sets ablaze ochre stones of ramparts creating magical atmosphere.
⏱️ 120 min
2
Tour de Constance, 131-foot cylindrical keep dominating Aigues-Mortes since 1248. The massive tower served as state prison from the 17th century, imprisoning Protestants after Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. The upper room with impressive vaults imprisoned Marie Durand for 38 years, who carved "resist" on the well rim. The 196-step spiral staircase leads to circular panoramic terrace. The visit evokes the prison-tower's history and moving testimonies of female captives. The monument symbolizes Huguenot resistance and religious intolerance.
⏱️ 90 min
3
Salins d'Aigues-Mortes, 26,700-acre salt exploitation producing Camargue salt. Salt pans in pink hues due to halophilic microalgae create spectacular colorful landscapes. Camelles, white salt mountains rising 65 feet, sparkle in sunlight. Thousands of pink flamingos frequent evaporation basins feeding on brine shrimp and algae. Discovery circuit by small train tours industrial and natural site explaining ancestral harvest. Boutiques sell artisanal salt—fleur de sel, grey salt, flavored—and derivative products.
⏱️ 120 min
4
Historic center of Aigues-Mortes, medieval bastide with preserved grid plan. Straight streets intersect at right angles following regular 13th-century urbanism. Place Saint-Louis bordered by café terraces honors the king-builder who departed on crusade from the port. Notre-Dame-des-Sablons church in southern Gothic style shelters relics and marine ex-votos. Limestone houses with colorful shutters align facades along paved streets. Artisan shops offer Camargue specialties: fougasse, gardiane, rice, salt.
⏱️ 90 min

Excursion in Gard Camargue discovering fauna and landscapes. Roads traverse expanses of reeds, rice paddies, marshes, and lagoons populated with birds. Pink flamingos, grey herons, egrets, ibis, and avocets allow observation from ornithological observatories. Herds of Camargue bulls and white horses graze in wet meadows. Traditional Camargue farmhouses dot preserved territory. Camargue Regional Natural Park protects 212,000 acres of internationally important wetlands. Changing light sublimes aquatic landscapes stretching to infinity.

Visit salt marshes by tourist train touring salt pans and evaporation basins. The 45-minute narrated circuit reveals seawater crystallization process from seawater pumping. Small wagons wind between salt mountains, pink and green basins according to saline concentration. Explanations detail mechanized harvesting, storage, and salt packaging. Stops allow photographing flamingos in surreal colored décor. Boutique offers tastings and purchases of locally produced flavored salts.

Taste gardiane de taureau at a typical Camargue restaurant. Traditional stew simmers Camargue bull meat AOC with red wine, black olives, garlic, and aromatic herbs. The rustic dish accompanied by Camargue rice IGP illustrates authentic regional gastronomy. Restaurants near ramparts also serve tellines, local shellfish, brandade, grilled fish. Vins des sables IGP produced on Camargue terroir pair with specialties. Fougasse d'Aigues-Mortes, brioche with orange blossom seeds, concludes traditional meal.
